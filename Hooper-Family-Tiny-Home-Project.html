    1. Refinance the FHA loan into a conventional loan (FHA doesn't allow LLC ownership)

🚨 Critical Sequencing — Read This Before Anything Gets Transferred

This tripped up the research and it's important: the homestead lot CANNOT be transferred into the LLC before the FHA loan closes. The bank needs the borrower to personally own the land they're building on. Here's the right order:

  1. Now: Darnell and Diane's smaller lot can go into LBH Properties LLC right away — it's not being used for FHA
  2. Before FHA application: The homestead lot must be deeded to Tianna personally — she's the FHA borrower. Tyra and Tony transfer the homestead to Tianna's name for the loan period.
  3. After 12 months: Tianna refinances FHA → conventional loan, then deed-transfers the homestead lot into the LLC. Both lots are now in LBH Properties together.

If both lots go into the LLC first, the FHA financing falls apart. The attorney needs to understand this sequence on day one. Tyra and Tony are temporarily transferring the homestead deed to Tianna for financing purposes — the operating agreement protects everyone's ownership rights during this period.

🏘️ 2. NC Workforce Housing Loan Program (NCHFA)

The NC Housing Finance Agency offers 0% interest, 30-year deferred loans for rental developments — but there's a catch worth knowing:

  • ⚠️ WHLP is NOT a standalone loan. You can only receive WHLP funds as part of a federal Housing Credits (LIHTC) application. You don't apply for WHLP separately.
  • Housing Credits are competitive and typically awarded to larger affordable housing developments (50+ units). A 4-6 unit project is unlikely to win a LIHTC allocation on its own.
  • 💡 Where this could work for us: If we partner with a developer or housing authority who is already pursuing Housing Credits in Brunswick County, our project could be included as part of a larger application.

⚖️ Rules & Zoning — What's Actually Legal
The Law That Makes This Possible

NC Senate Bill 495 (effective October 2025) requires every town to allow at least one ADU per residential lot. Navassa can't say no to 1 primary home + 1 ADU on each lot — that gives us 4 units without rezoning.

⚠️ Critical: 4 Units vs. 6 Units

SB 495 guarantees one ADU per lot = 4 units total (2 per lot). To get 6 units, the larger lot needs rezoning to multi-family or planned development. That's a 6–12 month process that requires Navassa board approval and a public hearing. Scenario A (4 units) works today. Scenario B (6 units) requires rezoning.

📐 Tiny Home Requirements in NC

  • Must be on a permanent foundation — on wheels = classified as RV, much harder to finance and permit
  • Must meet NC Residential Building Code — same as any house
  • Most FHA lenders require 400+ sq ft minimum — this is a lender overlay, not an FHA rule, but plan for 400–600 sq ft per unit to avoid financing complications
  • Each livable room: minimum 70 sq ft with 7 ft ceilings
  • ADU must be smaller than the primary home on the same lot

🏗️ Building Permits We'll Need (Per Home)

  • Building permit — with plans rated for 150 MPH wind (coastal zone)
  • Wastewater authorization — from Brunswick County Environmental Health
  • Separate trade permits — electrical, plumbing, mechanical (HVAC)
  • Zoning compliance verification — confirm residential zone allows intended use
  • Each unit needs a septic evaluation — licensed soil scientist required ($500–$1,000 per site)

⚠️ Event Venue & Short-Term Rental — Must Verify

Running a commercial event venue on residential land likely requires a special use permit or rezoning. Short-term rentals may need a permit from the Town of Navassa. Get both answers in writing from the Planning Department before investing in event space or listing on Airbnb.

🌻 Future Play — Agritourism Add-On (Phase 3)

Once the housing is cash-flowing, the larger lot's open space can support a small agritourism operation. This is a Phase 3 add-on — not the core business. Here's what would actually work in coastal NC:

❌ Lavender Won't Work Here

Navassa's humid, hot coastal climate and acidic sandy soil are the opposite of what lavender needs. Most successful NC lavender farms are in the mountains/piedmont. Trying to force lavender in Brunswick County means fighting fungal disease, importing alkaline soil amendments, and installing drainage infrastructure — expensive and high-risk for a small plot.

✅ What DOES Thrive in Coastal NC

🛡️ Protecting the Land for Future Generations

🛡️ Why the LLC Matters — This Is How We Keep the Land

LBH Properties, LLC already exists with the NC Secretary of State — named for Lillie Bell Hooper, who started all of this, and whose daughter Stephanie Hooper carried it forward. We update the members and operating agreement — not start from scratch. The LLC carries their purpose: this land stays in this family. North Carolina hasn't adopted the Uniform Partition of Heirs Property Act yet, which means family land can still be forced into sale if one heir wants out. The LLC prevents that.

  • Prevents forced sales. No one person — and no one branch — can sell to an outsider without the other branch's agreement. This requires 100% consent, not majority vote.
  • Right of first refusal. If one branch ever wants out, the other branch gets the first right to buy their share at appraised value. No outside buyers unless the family declines.
  • Generational transfer. Membership interests pass to heirs within the same branch. Your kids inherit your branch's share. Their kids inherit theirs. No forced buyout on death.
  • No outside members. Nobody gets added to this LLC without unanimous consent from both branches. The land stays in the family — period.
  • Protects personal assets. The LLC separates personal finances from property liability. If someone sues the property, they can't touch personal bank accounts, homes, or cars.
  • Makes passing land to children simple. Transfer LLC shares instead of splitting deeds. No probate fights.

💡 How the Money Actually Works — Plain English

1️⃣ Tianna Puts In Startup Cash ($20K–$30K)

This money does NOT go to a lender. It pays for the things that have to happen before any bank will talk to us: legal paperwork, land surveys, appraisals, permits, and the first round of site clearing. Think of it as "getting the project ready to show the bank." Tianna's cash contribution is credited toward Branch 2's ownership share in the operating agreement.

2️⃣ Both Families' Land Becomes the Down Payment

When you buy a house, the bank wants you to put money down — usually 3.5% to 20% of the home's cost. We don't pay that in cash. Instead, because both branches of the family already own their lots free and clear, the bank counts the land's value as our "skin in the game." An appraiser says the combined land is worth $105K–$205K. That's our down payment. No additional cash leaves our pockets for this part.

3️⃣ The Bank Pays for Construction

With the land as collateral, the bank gives Tianna a construction loan. This money goes directly to the builder and contractors — not to us. The bank sends an inspector at each stage and releases money only when work is verified. When all 4 homes are built, the construction loan automatically converts into a regular mortgage (monthly payments).

4️⃣ Tenants Pay the Mortgage

Working professionals pay $1,800–$2,200/month to live in our units. That rent covers the mortgage payment, insurance, cleaning crews, landscaping, and still leaves money for the family. The tenants are essentially paying off our loan while we build equity on our own land.

5️⃣ What the Family Owns at the End

After the mortgage is paid off (25–30 years), the family owns 4–6 homes + an event venue on land that was already ours. No bank lien, no debt. The rental income becomes nearly pure profit. The property passes to the next generation through the LLC — no deed splits, no probate fights. And every July 4th, we come home to Navassa.

The Bottom Line

Family land we already own. Tianna's cash gets us started. The bank pays for construction. Tenants pay the bank back. The family keeps the property forever — and we come home every Homecoming.

📖 What These Terms Mean — Glossary

🏠 ADU — Accessory Dwelling Unit

A legal term for a smaller, separate home on the same property as a main house. Our tiny homes qualify as ADUs. NC SB 495 says every town must allow at least one ADU per lot — they can't say no.

🏦 FHA Loan — Federal Housing Administration Loan

A government-backed mortgage with low down payments (3.5%). The FHA doesn't give you the money — a regular bank does, but the government insures the loan, so the bank takes less risk and offers better terms. Requires an owner-occupant — one family member must live on-site for at least 12 months. That person also commits to managing the property and event bookings. Check FHA loan limits by county →

🔨 Construction Loan

A short-term loan that pays for building the homes. Money is released in stages as construction progresses (not all at once). When the homes are done, it converts into a regular mortgage. The land serves as collateral — the bank holds a lien on the land until the loan is paid off.

🏛️ CDBG — Community Development Block Grant

Federal money given to states, which re-grant it to towns for community improvements. The town applies, not individuals. It funds public infrastructure (roads, water, sewer), housing rehab, and economic development. At least 70% must benefit low-to-moderate income residents. NC CDBG program page →

🛡️ LLC — Limited Liability Company

A business structure that protects personal assets. If someone sues the property, they can only go after the LLC's assets — not your personal house, car, or bank account. Also makes it easy to split ownership, manage income, and transfer shares to the next generation. Our existing entity is LBH Properties, LLC — named for Lillie Bell Hooper, whose daughter Stephanie Hooper carried the land forward. NC Secretary of State business search →

🏘️ NCHFA — NC Housing Finance Agency

A state agency that provides loans and tax credits for affordable and workforce housing. Their Workforce Housing Loan Program offers 0% interest, 30-year deferred loans — but only as part of a federal Housing Credits (LIHTC) application, not as a standalone loan. Best path for a small project: partner with a developer already pursuing Housing Credits in the county.

📜 SB 495 — Senate Bill 495

North Carolina law passed in 2025 that requires all towns to allow at least one ADU per residential lot. Towns cannot require special zoning, extra parking, or conditional permits. Effective October 1, 2025. Read the full bill text (PDF) →

🔐 Collateral

Something of value you pledge to the bank to secure a loan. If you can't pay, the bank can take the collateral. In our case, the land is the collateral — the bank knows that even if everything goes wrong, they can sell the land to get their money back. This is why owning land free and clear is so powerful.

👷 Workforce Housing

Housing designed for working professionals who earn too much for "affordable housing" programs but still need reasonably priced options near their jobs. Traveling nurses, teachers, first responders, and military personnel are classic workforce housing tenants. Positioning as workforce housing unlocks grants and public support that vacation rentals never get.

🔍 Furnished Finder

A platform like Airbnb but specifically for travel nurses and medical professionals looking for furnished monthly rentals. Landlords pay $199/year to list (vs. Airbnb's 15% per booking). Average stay is 90+ days. furnishedfinder.com →

🗺️ Rezoning

Changing the legal classification of a piece of land. Our lots are probably zoned "residential" (single-family). To put more than 2 units on one lot, we'd need to rezone to "multi-family" or "planned development." This requires a public hearing, town board vote, and typically takes 6–12 months. Not guaranteed.
